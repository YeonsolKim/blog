\,\,\,\,The most defensible analysis is this:

Clause
→ Subject NP + Predicate VP

Subject NP:
this medicine

Predicate VP:
cured me of my cold

\,\,\,\,Inside the VP, the head is the verb cured, and the verb determines what kinds of complements can appear in the rest of the VP. The book explicitly states that the head verb largely determines what complements are permitted in the VP, and that some verbs license PP complements headed by a specific preposition. It gives examples like consist of, ask for, congratulate on, and says that such PPs count as complements because they are licensed by the verb rather than freely added as adjuncts. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0} :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

\,\,\,\,So for your sentence, a first-pass structure is:

- this medicine = Subject NP
- cured = head verb
- me = Direct Object NP
- of my cold = PP complement

That yields the clause pattern:

Subject + Transitive Verb + Direct Object + PP Complement

This matches the book’s general point that canonical clauses are not limited to NP objects and AdjP predicative complements; VPs can also contain PP complements, as in He congratulated her on her promotion or We asked for an adjournment. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

\,\,\,\,Now the important question is whether of my cold is an adjunct or a complement. Under this framework, it should be analyzed as a complement, not an adjunct, because complements are licensed by the head, whereas adjuncts are not restricted in that way. The book states this contrast clearly: complements depend on the head verb’s licensing properties, while modifiers do not. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3} Since cure in this sense selects the specific preposition of, of my cold patterns with the book’s prepositional-verb examples such as accuse her of fraud and consist of fruit and vegetables. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

\,\,\,\,So the structure can be represented more explicitly as:

[Clause  
 [NP this medicine]  
 [VP cured [NP me] [PP of [NP my cold]]]]

Here, the PP is headed by of, and inside that PP, my cold is the object of the preposition. The book explicitly says that prepositions are heads of PPs and that the NP following a preposition is its object. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

\,\,\,\,One subtle point is that of my cold is not a predicative complement. Predicative complements ascribe a property to a predicand, as in She felt happy or They took me for a fool. By contrast, of my cold does not predicate a property of me; it expresses what me was cured of. So this is not the pattern Subject + Verb + Object + PredComp, but Subject + Verb + Object + PP complement. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6} :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}

\,\,\,\,So the final analysis is:

- this medicine: Subject NP
- cured: head of VP
- me: Direct Object NP
- of my cold: PP complement licensed by cure
- my cold: Object of the preposition of

\,\,\,\,In short, the sentence is not best analyzed as “S + V + O” only. It is more accurately:

S + V + O + PP(complement)

and the full predicate is cured me of my cold.
